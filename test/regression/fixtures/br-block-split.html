Looking and Light

"A painting is a record of looking, and looking is never a neutral act."
— a teacher whose name the catalogue forgot to print

A masterpiece of pictorial drama need not announce itself with spectacle. More often it works by patient accumulation, asking the eye to slow down until the ordinary geometry of a room, a window, and a seated figure begins to feel charged with consequence. The longer one stands before such a picture, the more its quiet arithmetic of distances and glances seems to widen into a meditation on the relationship between people and the act of seeing them. Nothing is hurried, and nothing is wasted; every interval has been measured against every other, so that the whole surface reads as a single sustained breath rather than a sequence of separate incidents arranged for convenience.

The central figure occupies the composition the way a keystone occupies an arch, holding the surrounding pressures in suspension. Behind him a city extends in receding towers, flattened just enough to keep the wall of the picture intact, and a second presence hovers at the right like a passing reflection that has not yet decided whether to stay. The painter intercepts these asymmetrical gazes through his own steady looking, recording a pursuit of balance and capturing the fleeting moment of estrangement, suspense, clarity, and unity that defines the act of painting itself.

Colour does the structural work that line refuses to do. Broad fields of warm and cool are laid against one another so that the temperature of the surface, rather than any drawn edge, organizes the space. Where two zones meet, the boundary is negotiated by the eye and not by the brush, and this is precisely what gives the scene its restless equilibrium. The result is a stage on which form and feeling exchange roles continually, the one lending weight to the other until it becomes impossible to say where description ends and invention begins.

"The weakness of much painting now is that its interest is entirely in form and not at all in content," the artist once asserted, and the picture before us seems determined to prove the opposite. It is at once a story and a technical inquiry, a piece of theatre and a triumph of formal engineering. Through this double allegiance it elevates the purpose of the double portrait, locating its duality not only in its subjects but in the very handling of paint, so that the broken pairing of two figures becomes a medium for exploring how composition and narrative act upon one another.

Scale, too, is part of the argument. A picture of this breadth refuses to be taken in at a glance, and that refusal is deliberate, because the subject is not an event but a duration. One reads it the way one reads a long sentence whose meaning only settles at the final clause, each interval of wall and floor and sky postponing resolution until the eye has travelled the entire width of the canvas. By the time the gaze returns to its starting point it has been taught a new rhythm of attention, slower and more deliberate than the one it brought to the encounter, and the painting keeps that rhythm in reserve for anyone willing to begin again.

What lingers afterward is less the inventory of objects than the climate they share. A particular quality of light, neither morning nor evening but some negotiated hour of the mind, settles over everything and binds the disparate elements into a single mood. It is the kind of atmosphere that cannot be pointed to in any one passage of paint, because it lives in the agreement among them all, and removing a single tone would let the whole carefully tuned weather of the picture escape into ordinary daylight.

In this way the work illuminates the manner in which we observe, and therefore come to recognize, the people and things that punctuate our world. It does not resolve the tension it stages; it keeps that tension visible, alive, and unfinished, which is perhaps the most honest account a painting can give of what it means to look at another person and to be uncertain, even then, of what one has actually seen.
